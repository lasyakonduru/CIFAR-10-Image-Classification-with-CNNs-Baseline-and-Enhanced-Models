Convolutional Neural Networks

GOAL: In this project, we will build a multi-class classification algorithm to predict 10 different classes of the CIFAR-10 dataset using a Convolutional Neural Networks.

Background. CIFAR-10 (Canadian Institute For Advanced Research) is a collection of images with 10 different classes representing:

  1. airplanes

  2. cars

  3. birds

  4. cats

  5. deers

  6. dogs

  7. frogs

  8. horses

  9. ships and

  10. trucks.

CIFAR-10 is a set of images that can be used to teach a computer how to recognize objects.

The CIFAR-10 dataset consists of 60000 32x32x3 i.e. color images in above described classes, with 6000 images per class. There are 50000 training images and 10000 test images. I have learned more about this dataset here - https://www.cs.toronto.edu/~kriz/cifar.html

Since the images in CIFAR-10 are of low-resolution (32x32x3), this dataset can allow researchers to quickly try different algorithms to see what works. Various kinds of convolutional neural networks tend to be the best at recognizing the images in CIFAR-10.

Now in this project, I will be the head of ImAIgine, a studio specializing in AI solutions for industry. You have been contracted by a car manufacturing consortium to train a deep learning model to detect deer and prevent car crashes caused by deer crossings. According to Purdue University, there are 1.5 million deer-vehicle collisions in the US each year, resulting in 1 billion in vehicle damage, with the average claim for these collisions being 3,995 USD (see: https://www.purdue.edu/fnr/extension/deer-vehicle-collisions/).

The model will be integrated into an AI master model that will guide emergency systems in future car models.

We will train a classification model focused on image identification. The goal is to achieve the highest possible overall accuracy and the highest recall for the whatever category the uploaded picture is.

Baseline model building

Specifications are as follows:

  1. First convolutional layer: 64 filters (kernels) (3x3), ReLU as activation function, padding set to "same", and a MaxPooling layer (2x2) with padding.
  2. Second convolutional layer: 64 filters (kernels) (3x3), ReLU as activation function, padding set to "same", and a MaxPooling layer (2x2) with padding.
  3. Feedforward layer: one hidden layer with 120 neurons, ReLU as activation function.
  4. Compilation: Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001, categorical cross-entropy as the loss function, and accuracy as the performance metric.
  5. Train for 10 epochs with a batch size of 32 and a validation split of 10%.

Enhanced model

Specifications are as follows:

  1. First convolutional layer: 128 filters (kernels) (3x3), ReLU as activation function, padding set to "same". No MaxPooling.
  2. Second convolutional layer: 64 filters (kernels) (3x3), ReLU as activation function, padding set to "same", with a MaxPooling layer (2x2) and padding.
  3. Third convolutional layer: 32 filters (kernels) (3x3), ReLU as activation function, padding set to "same", with a MaxPooling layer (2x2) and padding.
  4. Feedforward layers:
  • First hidden layer with 128 neurons, ReLU as activation function, and Dropout set to 25%.
  • Second hidden layer with 64 neurons, ReLU as activation function.
  1. Compilation: Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001, categorical cross-entropy as the loss function, and accuracy as the performance metric.
  2. Train for 10 epochs with a batch size of 32 and a validation split of 10%.

Answering the questions

  1. Overall Accuracy on Test Set for Baseline and Improved Models

    The overall accuracy for the baseline model is around 65% on the test set-from the evaluation summary, where the accuracy is 0.65-for the enhanced model, it improves a bit to attain an accuracy of around 67%. Since such an improvement does indicate that the enhanced model may do somewhat better by adjusting such factors as adding layers, dropout for regularization, or architecture changes, see model summaries.

    Although small, the gain in accuracy depicts that the enhanced model will slightly generalize better on unseen data. This gain is modest, which, in fact, depicts that further tuning might be required, or probably different strategies may be taken to make a marked difference in the model's performance.

  2. Recall and Individual Accuracy for Trucks in the Enhanced Model

The recall metric, which basically tells how good the model is at identifying positive instances of the class, shows difference across classes. To be more precise, for trucks, the individual accuracy seems to be comparatively high; that can be made out by the number of truck images correctly classified in the confusion matrix. On the other hand, the high probability score close to 1.0 for correctly classified truck images on the visual output also hints that the model has done a good job in learning trucks.

Are These Results Acceptable?

These results are somewhat acceptable but may not be ideal. This enhanced model showed better accuracy, and the performance in finding trucks was appropriate. Overall, the 67% model accuracy shows there is room for further improvement. For an application that needs a high precision, further improvements will be required. Increasing the training data, doing augmentation of data, or fine-tuning methods could help in further improving the model performance on all classes.
